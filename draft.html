Intentional Self-harm Deaths (Suicide) in Australia

The profound impact of each life lost to suicide on families, friends, workplaces, and the wider community cannot be underestimated. Recent data from the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) reveals that approximately 9 lives are lost to suicide daily. It's estimated that over 10 million Australian adults are connected to someone who has died by suicide, and 1 in 2 young people are affected by suicide before reaching the age of 25. This highlights the urgent need for increased awareness and support surrounding this critical issue.

Suicide by State and Territory

In 2022, the map starkly emphasizes regional differences in suicide rates, with the Northern Territory at 19.6 per 100,000, underscoring the need for urgent interventions. Conversely, New South Wales and Victoria at 11.2 and 11.4 respectively shed light on potential factors and stress the importance of support systems.

Analyzing data from 2012 to 2022, a consistent pattern emerges. The Northern Territory consistently records the highest suicide rate, emphasizing the ongoing need for substantial intervention and support. Conversely, New South Wales and Victoria consistently exhibit the lowest rates, suggesting potential successful strategies. This longitudinal view provided by the visualization enhances our understanding of the evolving suicide landscape, reinforcing the necessity for targeted efforts in prevention and support across Australia.

Age-standardised Suicide Rates

In order to measure changes over time, age-standardised suicide rates are presented in the graph on the left. Australians aged between 35 to 54, and 85 and above were more likely to die by suicide than those in other age cohorts. The highest death rate of 21.3 was achieved by the 45 to 54 year-old age group at 2015.

Risk Factors

Given that individuals can have multiple risk factors simultaneously, it's important to note that the proportion of suicide deaths attributed to each specified risk factor may be counted in more than one category, resulting in a distribution of percentages across various risk factors. As a result, the cumulative percentage for all risk factors in a given year may exceed 100%.

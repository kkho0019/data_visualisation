Intentional Self-harm Deaths
(Suicide) in Australia

The profound impact of each life lost to suicide on families, friends, workplaces, and the broader community is significant. Recent data from the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) reveals that approximately 9 lives are lost to suicide daily.

It's estimated that over 10 million Australian adults are connected to a suicide victim, and 1 in 2 young people are impacted by suicide before age 25, underscoring the pressing need for heightened awareness and support for this critical issue.

Suicide by State and Territory

Tooltip: 100K = 100,000

Tip: Slide to view data for each year.

Analyzing the dataset spanning from 2013 to 2022, a consistent pattern comes to light. The Northern Territory consistently reports the highest suicide rate, underlining the ongoing need for substantial intervention and support. Conversely, New South Wales and Victoria consistently exhibit the lowest rates, suggesting potential successful strategies in these regions.

Risk Factors

The heatmap shows the proportion of different risk factors in suicide. It is evident that depressive episodes consistently rank first as the most prevalent risk factor in suicide from 2018 to 2022, followed by suicidal thoughts and self-harm history. Do note that individuals may experience multiple risk factors at once, so cumulative percentage can exceed 100%.

Age-standardised Suicide Rates

Tip: Click an age range in the legend to view specific data.

This multi-line chart measures changes in age-standardised suicide rates over time. Australians aged between 35 to 54, and 85+ were more susceptible to suicide. The highest death rate of 21.3 corresponds with those aged 45 to 54 years in 2015. The age group 0-14 years is not displayed here because the maximum rate is below 1.

Suicide by Year and Sex

The stacked bar chart vividly displays the gender-wise distribution of suicide-related deaths over time. It reveals a pattern where a notably higher number of males die by suicide in contrast to females. In fact, female deaths represent less than 30% of the total, underscoring a stark gender disparity.

Mechanism of Death

The most common mechanism of death is by hanging, with a total of 1940 deaths. This is followed by poisonings and other methods. The least common mechanism is death by drowning and submersion. This indicates a need for more effective prevention strategies to deter individuals from engaging in such activities.

Finding Help and Support

24-hour telephone assistance is available through:

  • Lifeline - 13 11 14
  • Suicide Call Back Service - 1300 659 467
  • MensLine Australia - 1300 789 978
  • Kids Helpline - 1800 551 800
  • StandBy - Support After Suicide - 1300 727 247
  • National Alcohol and Other Drugs Hotline - 1800 250 015

If you, or someone you know is in immediate danger, please call 000, visit your nearest hospital or call any of the above services.
